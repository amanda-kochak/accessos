Emergency services are not accessible to the deaf and hard of hearing community and they’re not equitable towards people with mental or behavioral health issues. Traditional 911 emergency services can be particularly dangerous for people at the intersection of these two identities. To address the challenge, our team partnered with accesSOS, a nonprofit organization that aims both to make emergency services accessible to people incapable of calling 911, and to ensure people have alternative options other than 911 for emergency response based on their needs.

We set out to learn about the emergency services domain, understand how diverse populations interact with the services, and how we can develop a trusted solution that meets their needs. Following are some startling findings we uncovered about equity in emergency response in the United States.

37 million Americans can't hear or speak out loud.

Despite this, 70% of 911 call centers in the US are unable to receive text messages. The legacy equipment these call centers use is expensive and difficult to replace, and can’t receive text messages or detect location from mobile devices. This leaves deaf and hard of hearing people with often less-than-optimal methods for contacting 911 when they are in the throes of an emergency situation.

1 in 10 fatal police encounters have involved an individual with a known mental illness.

Nationally, in 2021 alone police fatally shot 104 people in the throes of a mental health crisis. In the majority of the country, police have little training to deal with situations involving people with mental illness and/or are deaf.

7-10% of US police contacts involve persons with mental illnesses, with a disproportionate number of these encounters resulting in arrest

Offenses tend to be minor, such as trespassing or disorderly conduct. An estimated 2 million people with mental illness are incarcerated nationwide every year.

An estimated 44% of people in jail and 37% of people in prison have a mental health condition.

And 10 - 25% suffer severe mental health concerns such as schizophrenia. Correctional facilities have become de-facto mental health institutions, yet often don’t provide the treatment people really need.

When people think of emergency services in the United States, they think of 911. People call 911 for assistance in the event of a fire, a medical emergency, or a crime. But sometimes, it isn’t possible for someone to verbally interact through a call. Other times, interaction with 911 really isn’t the right solution for certain types of crises. accesSOS’ initial design only enabled contacting 911 (medical, fire, and police), but as we dug deeper into the problem space, our perception of the solution evolved.

People from different cultural and racial backgrounds and with different abilities have different experiences with 911. Let’s take a moment and explore how experiences may differ based on an individual’s identity.

Sean is a great teenage son, but he lives with a mental illness.

One day, he goes for a walk around the neighborhood.

All of a sudden, something triggers him and he gets really upset in the middle of the street.

Someone in a house nearby sees him, and not knowing him, she decides to call the police.

Sean is upset and doesn't respond to the police the way they tell him to. They end up arresting him for being combative. No one ever stops to talk to him and figure out what's wrong.

Alice is young adult and is deaf.

One night, she drives home from a get together with friends.

She skids off the road, and hits a tree. She's hurt.

Since she is deaf, she can't call 911 directly for help. She first connects with her local video relay service. But when she props her phone on the steering wheel so that she can use both her hands, it falls off.

Alice finally gets her call through to 911, but it took a full 10 minutes longer than a direct phone call.

All of these people have different experiences reaching out to or being the subject of a 911 call. We can’t shape how emergency services respond in a situation. But we can design a system that ensures equal access to contacting emergency services, and that captures information likely to influence a more appropriate response. With this mission in mind, we set out to design for the progressive web app for accesSOS.

The debate around policing and 911 in the United States is active and robust. The issues are numerous, particularly in regards to racial inequities and the militarization of police. As such, we felt it necessary to narrowly focus on specific facets of the problem space.

Our research focused on two primary areas:

  1. Understanding user needs for a solution that enables them to contact both traditional emergency services (police, medical, fire) and mental crisis services.
  2. Understanding the current emergency services landscape at both social and infrastructural levels to be able to incorporate an app into the existing system.
